Reference data on muscle volumes of healthy human pelvis
and lower extremity muscles: an in vivo magnetic resonance
imaging feasibility study
Juliane Lube1 • Sebastian Cotofana2 • Ingo Bechmann1 • Thomas L. Milani3 •
Orkun Özkurtul4 • Tatsuo Sakai5 • Hanno Steinke1 • Niels Hammer1,5

Received: 13 February 2015 / Accepted: 22 July 2015
Ó Springer-Verlag France 2015

Abstract
Purpose Muscle volumes are of crucial interest when
attempting to analyze individual physical performance and
disease- or age-related alterations in muscle morphology.
However, very little reference data are available in the
literature on pelvis and lower extremity muscle volumes
originating from healthy and young individuals. Furthermore, it is of interest if representative muscle volumes,
covering large anatomical regions, can be obtained using
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) in a setting similar to
the clinical routine. Our objective was therefore to provide
encompassing, bilateral, 3-T MRI-based datasets on muscle
volumes of the pelvis and the lower limb muscles.
Methods T1-weighted 3-T MRI records were obtained
bilaterally from six young and healthy participants. Threedimensional volumes were compiled from 28 muscles and
muscle groups of each participant before the muscle volumes were computed.
Results Muscle volumes were obtained from 28 muscles
and muscle groups of the pelvis and lower extremity.
Volumes were larger in male than in female participants.

Introduction
Morphometric data on muscles are of principle interest to
determine intra- and inter-individual differences of physical performance and age-, disease-, or environment-related
alterations. Likewise, morphometric data on muscles can
be used to evaluate the effects of specific therapeutic
interventions in physiotherapy with a suspected impact on
muscle strength [5, 23, 28].
A commonly applied approach to estimate muscle
force is by obtaining muscle cross-sectional areas. Mostly,
measurements taken from standard anatomical planes are
used to determine cross-sectional areas of the lower limb,
resulting in anatomical cross-sectional areas (ACSA).
However, estimations on the muscle forces based on
ACSA vary depending on the slice thicknesses and the
interpolation between several slices. Therefore, the
resulting forces only give rough estimations. Physiological cross-sectional areas (PCSA), being measured in the

fiber direction of the muscle, are more precise for the
calculation of muscle forces than ACSA [18]. To increase
muscle force estimations to further extent, PCSA are
complemented by the pennation angle data of the
respective muscles [14, 30, 31]. Another approach to
estimate individual muscle force of the lower limb is
based on muscle volumes (MV). The MV can be understood as the sum of sarcomeres arranged both in parallel
and in series in the muscles’ architecture [17].
Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is a non-invasive
and X-radiation-free technique that provides three-dimensional data necessary to compute muscle volumes.
MRI provides reliable information on various types of soft
tissues for both post-mortem and living individuals. Previous studies utilizing MRI over extended body regions
were often based on post-mortem data or from single
individuals [25, 26]. The resulting data may therefore not
necessarily reflect the size and the functional shape of the
living, as anatomical fixation causes shrinkage and dehydration [31]. However, muscle data over extended
anatomical regions are hard to obtain in the living, since

the participants are forced to remain motionless for a
significant amount of time. Existing studies on living
participants were therefore mostly limited to narrowly
defined anatomical regions [2, 6, 7, 21, 22, 24, 27, 29].
Handsfield et al. recently presented an encompassing
dataset reaching from the hip to the toes, but this study
was limited to one side, therefore omitting potentially
relevant information for individuals’ bipedal physical
activity [12]. To date, there is no dataset available from
literature providing information on bilateral pelvis and
lower extremity MV. Such data, however, would help
improve one’s understanding of muscle function and
muscle involvement in pathogenesis, e.g., of the hip
abductor in hip joint disorders [9].
This feasibility study followed two aims. The first aim
was to apply state-of-the-art MRI imaging in a setting
similar to the clinical one to obtain MV data over an
extended body region. The second aim was to provide
bilateral pelvis and lower extremity MV datasets of healthy
young participants that can be used as a reference group by
the research community.

Magnetic resonance imaging
All participants were scheduled for the MRI scan at the
same time of day and were asked to avoid extensive
physical activity on the day prior to the investigation in
order to exclude potential effects that might influence the
imaging acquisition. The MRI scans were performed at the
Department of Radiology of the University Clinics of
Leipzig using a 3-T Magnetom TrioTim syngo (MR B17;
Siemens AG, Erlangen, Germany). The participants were
placed in prone position. Data of the pelvis and both legs
were obtained starting from the thoracolumbar transition to
the tips of both toes. A T1-weighted turbo-spin-echo
sequence was applied (TE = 11 ms, TR = 744 ms).
Scanning orientation was coronal with an inter-slice gap of
2.5 mm, slice thickness of 2.0 mm, and a resolution of
310 9 720 pixels. Total scanning time averaged 10 min
for each participant with the scan of the pelvis and both
legs being performed in a single session. Participants were
lying prone for at least 15 min before the scan started to
allow completion of fluid shifts during changing position
from upright to prone [4].
Segmentation process and MV calculation
MimicsÒ software (Materialise, Leuven, Belgium) was
used to calculate the MV from the T1-weighted MRI
datasets. The outline of each of the muscles was delineated
manually in a section-by-section manner using a graphic
tablet (Aiptec 600U Premium II, Aiptec Inc., Hsinchu,
Taiwan). Six trained experts with at least 2 years of
experience performed the segmentations (Fig. 1). A second
expert reader verified all segmentations for consistency in
all cases. The MV of each of the muscles was determined
with a software-based method, using numerical integration
of the segmented voxels. In a next step, three-dimensional
models were generated on the basis of the muscle outlines
using MimicsÒ software (Fig. 2). All muscles were segmented bilaterally. Segmentation and verification of each
participant’s dataset averaged 50 h. Intra-reader reliability
was 0.98 and the inter-reader reliability was 0.95 (Cohen’s
kappa).
Lower leg muscles were grouped into compartments
during segmentation process, according to their anatomical
and functional alliance: anterior compartment consisting of
the tibialis anterior, extensor digitorum longus, and
extensor hallucis longus muscle; lateral compartment
consisting of the fibularis brevis and longus muscle;
superficial posterior compartment consisting of the triceps
surae and plantaris longus muscle; and deep posterior
compartment consisting of the flexor digitorum longus,
flexor hallucis longus, and tibialis posterior muscle.

Discussion
The aim of the given study was to provide bilateral MV of
the human pelvis and lower extremity muscles obtained
from young and healthy individuals on the basis of a 3-T
MRI scanner in a clinical setting. The data on MV presented in this study are intended to serve as reference data
for the research community. The MV data obtained in this
study are in line with previous studies presenting data on
MV from active individuals [2, 7, 12]. Beyond this finding,
the MV presented here complement data from others on
the quadriceps femoris [2], triceps surae muscle [7], the
gluteus medius and minimus [8], and from both the dominant and the non-dominant sides [6, 12]. A principal
difference of the given study to others is that we used
images aligned in the coronal plane, whereas other studies
utilized axial images exclusively [1–3, 6–8, 10–12, 15, 16,
19, 20, 22, 24, 27, 29]. However, our MV data are similar
to the data published elsewhere [1–3, 6–12, 15–17, 20, 22,
24, 27, 29]. This similarity indicates that the method from
our setting is also feasible to obtain reliable results but in a
timesaving manner, as indicated by the short image
acquisition times. Furthermore, the coronal plane may be
advantageous concerning the origin and insertion sites of
the pelvis and lower extremity muscles being aligned in
the same manner.
The MV values of the quadriceps femoris obtained in
our study were similar to the data published in literature
regardless of varying participant numbers and of the
approach to data acquisition performed via segmentation or
estimation on basis of cross-sectional areas [3, 6, 12, 20,
24, 27, 29] (Table 4). Similar findings were found for the
hamstrings of both genders [6, 12, 20, 24, 27]. One
exception is the study of Krainski and coworkers [16], who
obtained significantly smaller MV, as compared to the
quadriceps femoris and hamstrings MV from our study.
This difference might be related to their approach, estimating MV by maximum cross-sectional areas [16]. Only
few studies exist on MV of the small muscles of the pelvis
[8, 11, 12, 22]. These MV data of the obturator internus
[22], gluteus maximus [11, 12], gluteus medius and minimus [8], and tensor fasciae latae [8, 11] are similar to our
results. However, none of the above-mentioned studies
presented MV of major muscles of the pelvis and lower
extremity from individual datasets, and only one of these
studies included the contra-lateral leg in their analyses [8].

123

Segmentation of small pelvic muscles such as the popliteus
muscle can result in larger inter-individual differences, as
these muscles can only be visualized in few slices of
standard MRI. Small alterations in muscle morphology
might consequently result in MV differences and thus
cause the observed differences among the given studies.
Handsfield et al. performed an encompassing study on the
right leg of healthy participants [12]. Their MV data were in
line with our findings (Table 4) [12]. Mersmann et al. presented MV data on the quadriceps femoris with much higher
MV magnitudes than in our study, which might potentially
be related to their participants being athletes only [19].
Fukunaga and coworkers found larger MV of the (rightsided) popliteus than in our study [10]. The differing maleto-female ratio between our study (1:1) and the work of
Fukunaga and colleagues (11:1) [10] or Handsfield et al.
(2:1) [12] might partially explain the differing results.
Another muscle with differing MV from Handsfield et al. is
the biceps femoris [12]. Although the reason for this difference remains unclear, our data are similar to those presented by Belavý and Konishi [3, 15], indicating a large
variation of the biceps femoris among different populations.
Similar deviations can be found for the semitendinosus in
comparison to others [3, 12, 15]. Concerning the MV data of
the lower leg, existing studies gave similar results compared
to our study [1, 3, 7, 10, 12, 20].
Previously, mostly 1.5-T scanners were used to obtain
MV on larger anatomical regions of the lower extremity
with the exception of Barnouin et al. [2], Csapo et al. [7],
and Handsfield et al. [12], using 3-T scanners as done in
our setup, but in the axial plane. Using higher magnetic
fields facilitated obtaining the MV with respect to the
imaging recording time and quality by means of standard
T1-weighted sequences in a setting similar to the clinical
routine. On the basis of these data, it will be of further
interest to determine the most representative ACSA or
PCSA for predicting muscle forces, as shown elsewhere
[6]. Such data could help optimize imaging routines over
extended body regions in a more timesaving manner. MRI
data from the standing position might give additional
information of the functional shape of the muscles, but
can currently only be collected experimentally, i.e., open
MRI in 0.2–1.2 T. A major limitation of the present study
is the small sample size, which certainly restricts the
options to analyze the obtained data. A greater sample
might have been beneficial in terms of generalization to
greater populations. However, the aim of this study was to
present a preliminary dataset on healthy references,
including the pelvis and both lower extremities on the
basis of an alternative MRI scanning protocol using 3-T
MRI.
Conclusively, this study provides the research community with encompassing, bilateral MV datasets of the

human pelvis and lower extremity obtained from young,
healthy, and active individuals in 3-T MRI. These data will
serve in future studies as a healthy reference for disease-,
age-, or training-related alterations, using an effective
approach for obtaining MRI-based data on muscles.
